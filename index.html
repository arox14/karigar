Why Karigar exists

Walk into a narrow gali in Sanganer and you'll hear the rhythmic thump-thump of a wooden block meeting fabric — that sound is centuries of muscle memory held in a single hand. In a quiet courtyard, a master of Blue Pottery leans over his chak, instinctively knowing the exact moment the glazed bartan is ready for the kiln's fire.

These moments of silent knowledge are what the modern economy misses. Jaipur's craftsmen are not disappearing because the skill is gone. They're disappearing because no system exists to make the skill visible, value it fairly, or ensure the next generation has a reason to learn it.

Karigar is building that system — through geo-tagged maps, intimate video archives, and interpretive paintings that show the labour behind the craft, not just the finished object.

Our story

Born from the pink-hued streets of Jaipur

Karigar was born from the shared observations of three students raised in Jaipur. Growing up surrounded by the city's craftsmanship, art was never separate from daily life — it existed in the streets, the homes, and the rhythm of the city itself. But over time, that rhythm began to change.
The realisation

Not a loss of skill.
A loss of recognition.

What we began to notice was not a loss of skill, but a loss of recognition. The mastery was still there — but the systems around it no longer saw or valued it in the same way.

Karigar emerged from this realisation: an effort to ensure that the soul of Jaipur's craftsmanship does not remain hidden or fade with time, but continues to exist as something visible, valued, and relevant in the modern world.

This is not a cultural problem. It is a structural market failure — where rare human skill exists, but cannot be seen, valued, or sustained within the systems that define economic worth.

The voices behind Karigar

Three perspectives.
One shared urgency.

Samarveer Singh Shekhawat
Co-Founder

Great-grandson of Padma Shri Shilp Guru Kripal Singhji Shekhawat — the father of Jaipur Blue Pottery. Always surrounded by the craft through the spaces, the process, and the legacy it carried. He noticed a persistent disconnect: while the final pieces were appreciated, the depth of skill and decades of mastery behind them remained largely unseen.

Trishiv Ohlan
Co-Founder

Observed how people engage with art at a surface level — focused on appearance rather than the effort, time, and precision behind the work. This gap between what is seen and what actually goes into creating it made him question how value is assigned, and why deeply skilled work is so easily overlooked. Karigar, for him, is about bridging that gap.

Anika Singh
Co-Founder

Motivated by understanding the uncertainty surrounding these crafts. While the work carries immense cultural and creative value, it does not always translate into stability for the artisans themselves. Seeing this imbalance made it clear that appreciation alone is not enough — there must be systems that support artisans in a real, sustainable way.

How we work

The Karigar ecosystem

01
The Digital Hub

The Karigar website serves as a first-of-its-kind Cultural Heat Map. We have digitally mapped nine traditional crafts across Jaipur, highlighting their specific clusters and current risk levels.

  • Artisan Registry — a curated directory of master practitioners
  • Teaching Archive — video interviews preserving at-risk knowledge
  • Context Capsules — history, origin, and cultural weight of each art form
  • Heat Map — spatial risk intelligence across Jaipur's craft clusters
02
The Physical Exhibition

The heart of our project is a collection of 36 Interpretive Paintings. These pieces don't just show the final product — they visualise the labour, the fragility, and the complexity of the artisanal process.

  • 36 paintings across all nine craft traditions
  • QR codes linking each painting to the artisan's story
  • Non-profit — proceeds support featured artisans directly
  • Designed to travel — each venue is a new audience
The problem we're solving

A structural
market failure.

"Jaipur's artisans are not disappearing due to a lack of skill, but due to a lack of recognition within modern economic systems."

Modern economies are designed to value speed, volume, and repeatability. Traditional crafts require time, precision, and deep tacit knowledge — forms of expertise that exist in practice rather than documentation. As a result, work that is rare and complex is often undervalued.

Buyers are unable to accurately judge the effort, skill, or rarity behind a product, allowing intermediaries to dominate pricing. Artisans become price-takers, earning disproportionately less than the worth of their work.

As many crafts now have only a few master practitioners left, the risk of extinction is immediate rather than gradual. Since most of this knowledge is not formally documented and is passed down through practice, its loss is often permanent once these artisans are gone.
